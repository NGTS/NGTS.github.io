Three more long period planets with NGTS!

March 2026: Following the publication of the recent paper on the confirmation of three long period transiting planets with NGTS led by Alicia Kendall, we have reached a monumental 100 refereed NGTS publications! Among these publications are 3 Nature and 2 Nature Astronomy papers, and the discoveries of 37 transiting planets and brown dwarfs, 9 noteworthy eclipsing binaries, as well as involvement in many other discoveries. We’d like to share a couple of planet highlights. NGTS-1b was the first planet discovered by NGTS and came as a complete surprise as such massive planets were not thought to exist around such small stars. At the time of discovery, NGTS-1b was in fact the most massive planet orbiting a low mass star! Sitting at the other end of the size scale we have NGTS-4b, nick-named ‘the Forbidden Planet’, which was the first Neptune discovered in the Neptune Desert and at the time of publication, is the shallowest transit discovered by a ground-based telescope! Another special planet is NGTS-11b; this is the first planet that was originally just a single-transit from NASA’s Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) mission and is one of the coolest known transiting gas giants. NGTS exoplanet discoveries have also spanned a range of orbital periods, from NGTS-10b which at the time of discovery was the shortest period ($\sim$0.77 days) hot Jupiter, all the way to NGTS-30b and NGTS-36b which have periods more than 100 days! We are now focussing our efforts on long-period planets and our 100th paper (Kendall et al. 2025) showcases three planets all with orbital periods longer than 25 days, one of which is Neptune-sized. Reaching this huge milestone of 100 refereed publications is all thanks to the hard work of all members of the NGTS consortium and we look forward to continuing to share and celebrate our discoveries! Here’s to the next hundred!
