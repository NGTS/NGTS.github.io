ABOUT

The Next-Generation Transit Survey (NGTS) facility consists of an array of twelve telescopes designed to make the highest-precision ground-based photometric observations of exoplanet transits. Our scientific focus is finding and characterising planets that are suitable for detailed study with large facilities, particularly those of ESO, which are co-located with NGTS in Chile. The NGTS facility is described in more detail here.

NGTS is located at the ESO Paranal Observatory in Chile, which also hosts the Very Large Telescope (VLT) and neighbours the Cerro Armazones site of the Extremely Large Telescope (ELT). This is the best site in the world for our science, with an exceptional 78% of photometric nights, and extremely dry conditions that minimises noise at red wavelengths due to water absorption. NGTS was the first non-ESO facility granted access to Paranal, reflecting how our high-precision photometry complements the capabilities of ESO facilities. ESO provides generous in-kind support to NGTS, including a public data archive that provides access for the wider astronomical community.

The NGTS facility was constructed by a consortium of six institutes, including four UK universities: Warwick, Cambridge, Leicester and Queen's Belfast, together with Geneva Observatory and Berlin planetary science group of the German Aerospace Centre (DLR), which is the PI institute for the ESA PLATO mission. The consortium also includes representatives of the Chilean astronomical community and we have been joined by scientists from many other institutes, including Queen Mary University of London, University of Oxford, Universty of Sheffield, University of Southampton, the Open University and Armagh Observatory. The capital costs of the facility were met by the consortium institutes, and operations are supported by the UK Science and Technology Facilties Council (STFC). The NGTS facility is operated from the University of Warwick, UK, and the principal investigator is Prof. Peter Wheatley.
